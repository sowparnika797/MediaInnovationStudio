Journeys of Hope: Understanding the Refugee Experience in the U.S.

“The problem is not just me and my kid. Not just that refugee and the kids. The problem is my whole country” – says Adiba Matin a refugee from Afghanistan-In a world increasingly marred by conflict, persecution, and climate change, millions of individuals and families find themselves forced to flee their homes, seeking safety and a better life in foreign lands. The United States, a nation built on the principles of freedom and opportunity, remains a key destination for many refugees. Yet, the journey to this new life is often fraught with challenges, as these individuals carry with them not only the burdens of their pasts but also the hope for a brighter future.

A refugee is a person who has been forced to flee their country due to fear of persecution, war, or violence. Megan Hecht an attorney practicing at the Justice center in Massachusetts says "As I explain, the legal side of refugee status and resettlement in the U.S. is governed by the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), which defines a refugee under section 101(a)(42). To qualify for refugee status, you must show that you have a well-founded fear of persecution in your home country based on one or more protected grounds—like race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group, such as gender or sexual orientation. Additionally, your home country’s government must be either unable or unwilling to protect you, and the U.S. government must determine that resettlement is appropriate because you cannot safely relocate within your own country.

Upon arrival, refugees encounter numerous obstacles, with language barriers being a major issue. As Adiba shares, "The language problem would not just be with me, but with my husband and my kids. They do not understand the language. They do not understand the culture." Nearly 45% of refugees resettled in the U.S. in 2019 spoke limited English, complicating communication and access to essential services. To address this, organizations often sign refugees up for English as a Second Language (ESL) programs, as Kathryn Waidler, the Director of Sponsorship at the International Institute of New England, explains: "We also sign them up for English for Speakers of Other Languages, ESL. They get assessed and placed in a class depending on their level. They're usually in specialist services for about 90 days, and then they get employment until they find a job, with ESL support available as long as they want it." However, Kathryn also notes, "Learning English is a huge challenge. Often, because refugees need to get jobs right away to pay their rent, they don't have time for classes." Finding meaningful employment is another barrier, as jobs available to refugees are often low-paying and lack long-term stability. This linguistic and economic hurdle can lead to isolation, while cultural adjustment adds further challenges as refugees navigate unfamiliar social norms and try to preserve their identities. Community organizations provide vital support to help them through this transition. “So that’s a lot of difference actually. But I was aware of the cultural difference between my language, which became my culture, and the American culture,” says Meron Kleftsiyon, a refugee from Eritrea. Cultural adjustment plays a crucial role in the refugee experience, as adapting to a new culture while preserving one’s identity can be a delicate balance. Refugees like Meron must navigate unfamiliar social norms, values, and customs, which often create feelings of dislocation and anxiety. Community organizations play a critical role in this process, offering support and helping refugees connect with others who share similar backgrounds and experiences. These networks are vital for fostering a sense of belonging and community, which can significantly ease the transition into American society.
